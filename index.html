The Investment Game

Original Game Strategy

You are given $1,000 to play this game: Flip a coin each year until age 55. If heads, increase your balance by 50%. If tails, reduce your balance by 40%.

Modified Game Strategy

Same as above, but you must bet exactly 50% of your current balance on each flip.

Questions to Answer

75% Grade

  1. Expected Value Analysis: What is the expected value after 1 coin flip for the original game?
Expected Value Analysis (1 coin flip):
=====================================
Initial balance: $1000
Heads outcome (50% gain): $1500
Tails outcome (40% loss): $600
Probability of heads: 0.5
Probability of tails: 0.5

Expected Value = P(Heads) × Heads_Outcome + P(Tails) × Tails_Outcome
Expected Value = 0.5 × $1500 + 0.5 × $600
Expected Value = $1050

Answer: The expected value after 1 coin flip is $1050

Discussion of Results:

The game initially seems to be a good investment, but the expected value is only a theoretical average. In reality, the game is much riskier than it first appears. While the expected value is 1050, which is a 5% gain per flip, the actual outcome is much more volatile. Since the average tells you only the results of playing many times in the long run, it is not a good indicator of the actual outcome of a single game.

  1. Expectation vs. Reality: Is the expected value positive or negative? Do you expect your account to be worth more or less than $1,000?
Expectation vs. Reality Analysis:
===============================
Initial balance: $1000
Expected value after 1 flip: $1050
Expected difference: $50
Expected return: 5%
✓ The expected value is POSITIVE
✓ You expect your account to be worth MORE than $1,000
✓ Expected gain: $50 (5%)

Key Insight:
The positive expected value suggests this game is theoretically favorable.
However, this is just the average outcome - your actual result could be very different!

Discussion of Results:

On paper, the game looks enticing since the expected value is $1,050, or a 5% gain per flip. That suggests players should come out ahead on average. But averages only hold when you can play many many times. In reality, you only get one sequence of flips until age 55, and that exposes you to the full volatility of the rules. That being losing a few games can quickly drag your balance down, while on the other hand, a lucky run could make you thousands of dollars. The real lesson is that expected value shows the average, not the risk. In high-variance situations like this, you have to think about the whole range of possible outcome not just the average which can be deceiving.

Comments on the Results:

The comparision between the modified strategy and the original strategy highlighed super interesting results regarding risk managment. The modified strategy, which only risks 50% of the current balance on each flip, shows significantly different characteristics compared to the original strategy that risks 100% of the balance.

The modified strategy demonstrates lower volatility and more conservative outcomes, with a lower spread of outcomes. However, this comes at the cost of lower expected returns and reduced probability of exceeding the 1,000 dollar mark and achieving the high-reward outcomes that make the original strategy potentially beneficial.

This comparison highlights an interesting and fundamental trade-off in investment strategy design: risk reduction comes at the cost of potential upside. The modified strategy provides more consistent, predictable outcomes but sacrifices the possibility of extreme gains that the original high-risk approach provides. This comparison is value for seeing how different risk management approaches can alter the probability distribution of investment outcomes.
